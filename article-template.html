Why Your Water Pressure Drops When Someone Flushes the Toilet

That sudden blast of cold water in your shower isn't a mystery — it's your plumbing system telling you something. A licensed plumber with nearly 50 years of experience explains what's actually happening, and when it's time to fix it.

If you've ever been in the shower and felt the water suddenly go cold — or scalding hot — right when someone flushed a toilet nearby, you know the problem. It's one of the most common complaints homeowners have, and in most cases it has a straightforward explanation.

The short answer: your home's water supply pipes are undersized, partially restricted, or your pressure balancing valve is worn out. But the specific cause matters, because the fixes are very different.

How Your Home's Water Supply System Works

Your home's plumbing operates on a relatively simple principle: water comes in from the street at a certain pressure, travels through a main supply line, and then splits off into branches that serve different fixtures. The whole system shares that one incoming supply.

When a toilet flushes, it opens a fill valve and demands a significant volume of water quickly — typically a gallon or more in a short burst. If your supply pipes are undersized or there's any restriction in the system, that sudden demand pulls pressure away from other fixtures running at the same time.

Your shower is especially sensitive to this because it relies on a precise mix of hot and cold water. When cold pressure drops suddenly, you get more hot water — and the temperature swings dramatically.

The Most Common Causes

1. Undersized or Corroded Supply Pipes

Most homes built before 1980 used ¾-inch galvanized steel pipe for main supply lines. Over decades, mineral deposits build up inside these pipes, dramatically reducing their effective diameter. A pipe that started at ¾ inch can be functionally closer to ⅜ inch after years of buildup — cutting water flow by more than half.

This is the most common cause in older homes and the one most homeowners don't discover until they're already dealing with multiple problems.

⚠️
Warning Sign
If your pressure problem has gotten noticeably worse over the past few years, and you have an older home with galvanized pipes, you likely have buildup. This will continue to worsen until the pipes are replaced.

2. A Worn Pressure-Balancing Valve

Showers installed since the 1990s are required by code to have a pressure-balancing valve (sometimes called a "scald guard"). This valve is designed to automatically compensate when cold or hot pressure changes — keeping the temperature steady even when a toilet flushes.

If yours is doing its job, you shouldn't feel much of a temperature change. If you're feeling a dramatic swing, the balancing valve may be worn out or clogged with mineral deposits.

3. A Partially Closed or Restricted Shutoff

Shutoff valves that have been partially closed — either accidentally or after a repair — can create enough restriction to cause pressure drops under demand. Check the main shutoff and any branch shutoffs serving your bathroom.

4. Low Municipal Water Pressure

If your incoming pressure is already at the low end (below 45 PSI), any demand on the system will be more noticeable. You can test your incoming pressure with an inexpensive gauge that attaches to a hose bib — a worthwhile $15 investment before spending money on other fixes.

How to Diagnose Your Specific Problem

Before calling a plumber, run through this quick diagnostic sequence. What you find will tell you whether this is a DIY fix or a professional job.

  1. Test your incoming pressure. Attach a pressure gauge to an outdoor hose bib. Normal range is 45–80 PSI. Below 45 means low municipal pressure; above 80 means your pressure reducing valve (PRV) may be failing.
  2. Check all your shutoff valves. Main shutoff at the meter, and the individual shutoffs under sinks and at the water heater. Make sure they're fully open.
  3. Note whether the problem is temperature, pressure, or both. Temperature-only swings usually point to the shower valve. Pressure drops at multiple fixtures point to the supply system.
  4. Check the age of your pipes. If you have galvanized steel pipe and the home is over 40 years old, you likely have buildup regardless of other symptoms.
💡
Pro Tip
Run your kitchen faucet at full blast and flush the toilet at the same time. Watch how much the kitchen flow drops. A significant drop (more than 20–30%) at a fixture far from the toilet suggests a whole-system supply issue, not just a shower valve problem.

What You Can Fix Yourself vs. When to Call a Pro

🔨
DIY-Friendly
  • Testing incoming water pressure with a gauge
  • Opening a partially closed shutoff valve
  • Replacing a shower pressure-balancing cartridge (most brands)
  • Cleaning a clogged showerhead or aerator
⚠️
Call a Plumber
  • Replacing galvanized supply lines (whole-house repiping)
  • Adjusting or replacing a pressure reducing valve (PRV)
  • Diagnosing pressure loss across multiple fixtures
  • Any work involving the main supply line or meter
🛑
Don't Ignore This
If you have galvanized pipes that are 40+ years old, the pressure drop is the least of your concerns. Those pipes are likely also corroding from the inside, which can contaminate your water supply and will eventually fail. Get them inspected.

Repair Options and Typical Costs

Once you've identified the cause, here's what you're looking at in terms of repairs and realistic costs.

  • Shower pressure-balancing cartridge replacement: $15–40 in parts, DIY-friendly. A plumber will charge $100–200 labor if you hire out.
  • Pressure reducing valve replacement: $50–150 in parts. Requires shutting off the main and soldering or pressing new fittings — hire a plumber for $200–400 total.
  • Whole-house repipe (galvanized to copper or PEX): $4,000–15,000 depending on home size and accessibility. This is the permanent fix for older homes with buildup. PEX is now the preferred material — flexible, freeze-resistant, and faster to install.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this a code violation in my home? ▾
Not necessarily. Older homes were built to the codes of their time, and undersized pipes were acceptable practice for decades. However, if you're doing any remodeling work that requires permits, updated code may require you to bring affected systems up to current standards.
Will a water softener fix my pressure problem? ▾
No. A water softener addresses water quality, not supply capacity or pipe condition. It won't fix buildup that's already there, and it won't increase the effective diameter of corroded pipes. It can help slow future buildup in new pipes, but it's not a fix for existing pressure issues.
My home is newer — why do I still have this problem? ▾
Newer homes can still have this issue if the pressure-balancing valve in the shower is worn or poorly installed, if there's a partially closed shutoff somewhere in the system, or if the home was built with undersized branch lines to certain fixtures. It's worth having a plumber assess the shower valve specifically.
How long does a whole-house repipe take? ▾
Most single-family homes can be repiped in 2–4 days with PEX. The work requires opening walls in some areas, but experienced crews minimize the number of access points. You'll be without water for portions of each work day but not overnight.

Key Takeaways

  • Pressure drops during flushing are caused by supply competition — all fixtures share one incoming line.
  • The most common cause in older homes is galvanized pipe with decades of mineral buildup inside.
  • A worn pressure-balancing valve in the shower causes temperature swings, not true pressure loss.
  • Test your incoming pressure first — it's a $15 fix and tells you a lot about your system.
  • Galvanized pipes over 40 years old should be on your radar for replacement regardless of symptoms.
